Tier 2 — canonical vs standard dictionary Sanskrit

Here the slide differs from dictionary-standard Sanskrit (short vowels, a dropped virāma). Your rule says the canonical governs — but because this is a teaching tool, each is yours to confirm. References open the relevant dictionary entry.

Verse 4“upward / above”decide
Our app (standard)ऊर्ध्वंūrdhvaṁ (long ū)
Canonical slideउर्ध्वंurdhvaṁ (short u)

Context: “ऊर्ध्वं रक्षतु मे विद्या” — May the Vidyā guard me above. Dictionary form is ऊर्ध्व (ūrdhva, long ū); the slide shortens it.

Refwisdomlib: ūrdhva

My take: follow canonical — a common vowel-shortening, and matching the chant matters more here.

Verse 6Nīla-Sarasvatī (the Blue Sarasvatī)decide
Our app (standard)नीलसरस्वतीnīlasarasvatī (nīla = blue)
Canonical slideनिलसरस्वतीnilasarasvatī (short i)

Context: “श्रोत्रे निलसरस्वती” — Nīla-Sarasvatī (a form of Tārā) in the ears. The name means blue (नील, nīla, long ī); the slide shortens to निल.
Note: this reverses the #4 board’s earlier “keep ours” — that call predated the canonical authority.

Refwisdomlib: nīlawisdomlib: Nīla-Sarasvatī

My take: this one is the weakest case for the slide — नील = “blue” is the deity’s actual name. Reasonable to keep standard. Your rule still says canonical; your call.

Verse 11“…blood, flesh, bone, marrow, etc.”decide
Our app (grammatical)रक्तमांसास्थिमज्जादीन्…majjādīn (acc. pl., virāma)
Canonical slideरक्तमांसास्थिमज्जादीन…majjādīna (no virāma)

Context: the object of “पातु देवी शवासना” — May the corpse-seated Goddess guard [my] blood, flesh, bone, marrow, and the rest. Grammar wants the accusative plural मज्जादीन् (majjādīn, with virāma); the slide drops the virāma, which reads majjādīna. This is the one spot the slide looks like a scribal slip.

Refwisdomlib: majjā (marrow)

My take: keep grammatical — a missing virāma is almost certainly a slide typo, and the study tool shouldn’t teach it. Flagging for your call.
